The Circle of Fifths: Your Guide to Musical Keys

The Circle of Fifths is one of music’s most powerful tools. It shows how all 12 notes and keys in music are connected, making it easier to understand, read, and write music. Whether you’re a beginner or a pro, this guide will help you unlock the secrets of the circle!

What is the Circle of Fifths?

The Circle of Fifths is a diagram that arranges the 12 notes of the chromatic scale in a circle. Each step around the circle moves up by a musical interval called a fifth. It helps musicians see which keys are related, how sharps and flats work, and how to change keys smoothly. This page is a visual and educational guide—no sound or interactivity required.

Understanding Keys & Intervals

  • What is a key? A key is a group of notes that sound good together. Each key has a “home” note (the tonic).
  • What is a fifth? A fifth is the distance between one note and the note five steps higher. For example, from C up to G is a fifth.
  • Major vs. Minor: Major keys sound bright and happy. Minor keys sound more mellow or sad.

How to Read the Circle of Fifths

  1. Start at the top: C Major (no sharps or flats).
  2. Move clockwise: Each step adds a sharp. (G, D, A, E, B, F♯)
  3. Move counterclockwise: Each step adds a flat. (F, B♭, E♭, A♭, D♭, G♭)
  4. Inner circle: Shows the relative minor for each major key.

Keys next to each other are closely related and sound good together!

Major & Minor Keys: How They Relate

Every major key has a relative minor key that shares the same notes. For example, C Major and A minor use the same notes but start on different tonics.

  • Relative minor: Find the minor key inside the circle, just below the major key.
  • Parallel minor: Has the same tonic note, but different notes (C Major → C minor).

Sharps & Flats Around the Circle

  • Each step clockwise adds a sharp (#) to the key signature.
  • Each step counterclockwise adds a flat (♭).
  • Opposite sides of the circle are the most different (C Major vs. F♯ Major).

Example: G Major has 1 sharp (F#), D Major has 2 sharps (F#, C#), and so on.

How to Use the Circle in Real Music

Transposing Songs

Want to play a song in a different key? Move the chords around the circle by the same number of steps.

Writing Chord Progressions

Chords next to each other on the circle sound great together. Try C → G → Am → F (used in many pop songs!).

Improvising & Harmonizing

Use the circle to find which keys and chords fit together for solos or harmonies.

Famous Example

  • Let It Be (The Beatles): C → G → Am → F
  • Canon in D (Pachelbel): D → A → Bm → F#m → G → D → G → A

Glossary & Resources

Key
A group of notes that form the basis of a piece of music.
Fifth
The interval between one note and the note five steps higher.
Relative Minor
The minor key that shares the same key signature as a major key.
Transposing
Changing the key of a song or piece of music.
